BLISSFUL BIHAR

, THE LAND OF MONASTERIES

    Bihar is a state in Eastern India. It is the second largest state by population, the 12th largest by area, and the 14th largest by GDP in 2024. Bihar borders Uttar Pradesh to its west, Nepal to the north, the northern part of West Bengal to the east, and Jharkhand to the south. Bihar is split by the river Ganga, which flows from west to east. On 15 November 2000, southern Bihar was ceded to form the new state of Jharkhand. Only 11.27% of the population of Bihar lives in urban areas as per a 2020 report. Additionally, almost 58% of Biharis are below the age of 25, giving Bihar the highest proportion of young people of any Indian state. The official language is Hindi, which shares official status alongside that of Urdu. The main native languages are Maithili, Magahi and Bhojpuri. But there are several other languages being spoken at smaller levels.

I am not going to make it long. I will just tell you about two big events of that year.

  • The Huygens probe was a robotic lander that successfully landed on Saturn's moon Titan in 2005, becoming the first successful landing on a world in the outer Solar System. Designed and built by the European Space Agency (ESA), it was launched aboard NASA's Cassini spacecraft and parachuted through Titan's atmosphere to the surface. The mission aimed to study Titan's atmosphere and surface, and the probe's data continued to transmit for over an hour after landing.
      • Key aspects of the Huygens probe:
      Mission Goal:
  • To study Titan's atmosphere and surface during descent and after landing.
      • Launch and Transit:
  • Huygens was launched in 1997 and hitched a ride on NASA's Cassini spacecraft to Saturn.
      • Descent and Landing:
  • The probe descended through Titan's atmosphere by parachute and landed on the frozen surface.
      • Scientific Instruments:
  • Huygens carried a suite of scientific instruments to measure atmospheric properties, wind speeds, and take images of the surface.
      • Data Transmission:
  • The probe transmitted data for 72 minutes after landing, providing valuable insights into Titan's environment.
      • Legacy:
  • Huygens's successful landing opened a new chapter in planetary exploration, particularly in the outer Solar System.
    • On May 6, 2005, a hybrid solar eclipse occurred. Hybrid eclipses, which are rare, transition between a total solar eclipse and an annular solar eclipse depending on the observer's location. This 2005 eclipse path started as annular, became total for a portion of the path, and then reverted to annular.
      Details of the 2005 Hybrid Eclipse:
      Rarity:
  • Hybrid eclipses are uncommon, accounting for only about 3.1% of solar eclipses in the 21st century.
      • Path:
  • The eclipse path traversed the Southern Hemisphere, starting southeast of New Zealand and crossing the Pacific Ocean to Panama, Colombia, and Venezuela.
      • Observer Locations:
  • Fred Espenak on a ship within the Moon's shadow track observed a total eclipse, capturing the solar corona and prominences. Stephan Heinsius near Penonomo Airfield, Panama, experienced an annular eclipse.
      • Visibility:
  • A partial eclipse was visible in a broader area, including parts of New Zealand, the South Pacific, South and North America.
      • Key Locations:
  • The total eclipse was not visible on land, but the annular eclipse was visible in the southern tip of Puntarenas Province of Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia, and Venezuela.
      • Why is it a hybrid eclipse?
    The Earth's curvature and the Moon's shadow path cause the type of eclipse to change along the path. In some locations, the Moon's shadow completely covers the Sun, creating a total eclipse. In other locations, the Moon only partially covers the Sun, resulting in an annular eclipse, where a bright ring of the Sun is visible around the Moon
